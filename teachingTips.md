### 1. Rename “groups” to disrupt autopilot  
When it’s time to form groups, call them something else — *clumps*, *squadrons*, *molecules*, *partners in crime* — anything but “groups.”

**Why this works:**  
Students have a behavioural script for “group work.” A small language shift interrupts autopilot, resets energy, and signals that this task is intentional — not routine.

---

### 2. Give instructions before forming groups  
Explain the task while everyone is still seated and attentive.  
Use: *“What I want each of you to do is…”* rather than *“What I want each group to do is…”*

**Why this works:**  
Once students start moving, cognitive attention drops. Giving directions to individuals before the social transition preserves clarity, reduces repetition, and increases accountability.

---

### 3. Start with a quick team-naming task  
Have each team choose a name as their first step. Use a simple prompt like:  
- Color + Reptile  
- Car Brand + City in Pakistan  
- Aquatic Mammal + Gemstone  

**Why this works:**  
A low-stakes identity task gets every voice involved immediately, reduces awkwardness, and builds a shared sense of ownership before the academic work begins.

---

### 4. Time-box every phase of the activity  
Break the task into clear, short segments (e.g., brainstorm, select, develop, prepare to report).

**Why this works:**  
Structure drives productivity. Clear time limits increase urgency, reduce drift, and keep momentum high.

---

### 5. Make time limits oddly specific  
Instead of “5 minutes,” try 4:48 or 5:12.

**Why this works:**  
“About five minutes” feels elastic. A precise time signals intentional design and heightens students’ awareness of the clock. Specificity increases focus.

---

### 6. Let roles emerge — then formalize them  
Allow groups to begin working for a few minutes before assigning or confirming roles. Once patterns of participation emerge, have students select or formalize responsibilities.

**Why this works:**  
Early interaction reveals natural strengths and dynamics. Assigning roles after brief collaboration makes the choices more authentic and increases buy-in, while still ensuring accountability.
